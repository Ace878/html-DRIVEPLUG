HISTORY OF ELECTRIC VEHICLES

2024: Electric Vehicles Take Over

Renault Scenic E-Tech

A compact crossover SUV that won the European Car of the Year 2024 award. It offers a range of over 385 miles, with options for 60 kWh and 87 kWh battery capacities.

The Renault Scénic Vision concept car, introduced in May 2022, features a unique design with no B-pillar and easy access to the passenger compartment. Built on the CMF-EV platform, it incorporates 70% recycled materials, including a floor made from recycled milk bottles and pipes, and seats crafted from polyester. Inside, it features adjustable screens and camera mirrors. The concept is powered by a 160 kW (210 hp) electric motor and a 40 kWh lithium-ion battery, supplemented by a 15 kW hydrogen fuel cell as a range extender, offering an estimated range of 800 km (500 mi).

The production model, released in September 2023, is available in two versions: the Standard range with a 60 kWh battery, 125 kW motor, and 420 km range, and the High range with a 160 kW motor, 87 kWh battery, and a range of over 620 km.

2020s: A Global Shift to Electric Vehicles

The 2020s saw a significant shift toward electric vehicles with the rise of major manufacturers like Tesla, Nissan, GM, and Volkswagen pushing for an electric future. The demand for EVs skyrocketed, with government incentives, expansion of charging infrastructure, and better battery technology accelerating the transition.

Several countries, including the UK, France, and Norway, have announced plans to phase out the sale of new gasoline and diesel vehicles by 2030-2040. Companies like Tesla continue to lead the market with models like Model 3 and Model Y, and the global sales of electric vehicles have surged dramatically.

2010s: Rise of Tesla and Mainstream EVs

The 2010s marked the introduction of electric vehicles for the mass market. Tesla’s success with the Model S, the Nissan Leaf, and other electric cars set the stage for an EV revolution. Charging infrastructure and battery ranges also improved during this time. Tesla helped to cement the viability of EVs for long-distance travel with the introduction of the Model S, which offered a range of over 300 miles on a single charge.

Other manufacturers, such as Chevrolet and BMW, introduced their own electric vehicles, and the market grew significantly. By the end of the decade, electric vehicles were no longer niche products but were seen as a mainstream alternative to gasoline cars.

1990s: The GM EV1 and California Mandates

The 1990s saw the first major push for electric vehicles with the introduction of the GM EV1. Despite its early success, the car was discontinued. However, it paved the way for more electric vehicle development as automakers responded to California's Zero Emission Vehicle (ZEV) mandate. The California Air Resources Board (CARB) passed regulations requiring automakers to produce zero-emission vehicles, which led to the development of cars like the EV1, which had a range of up to 80 miles per charge.

Despite the EV1's popularity among its users, GM eventually discontinued the model due to financial reasons, sparking debates over the future of electric cars. However, this era laid the groundwork for the resurgence of electric cars in the 21st century.

1900s: The Golden Age of Electric Vehicles

In the early 1900s, electric cars were quite popular and widely used in cities. They were quieter, cleaner, and easier to operate compared to gasoline-powered vehicles. In fact, in 1900, electric vehicles made up a significant portion of the automobile market. Companies like Detroit Electric and Baker Electric were major players in the electric vehicle market.

The rise of gasoline-powered vehicles, such as the Ford Model T, which was cheaper and had a longer driving range, led to the decline of electric cars by the 1920s. However, during this time, electric cars were favored by wealthy individuals and urban areas due to their smooth, silent operation.
